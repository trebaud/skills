# Cognitive science behind `i-know-kungfu`

The evidence base for the skill. Each principle has the finding, why it works, and **how to apply it** in a
session. The creed in `SKILL.md` is the compressed version; this is the why.

---

## 1. Testing effect / retrieval practice
**Finding (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006):** trying to *retrieve* information strengthens memory far more than
re-studying it. Students who were tested on material outperformed students who re-read it, especially on
delayed tests — even though re-reading *felt* more effective.

**Why:** retrieval is itself a memory-modifying event. Reconstructing a memory makes it more accessible next
time. Recognition (multiple choice) helps less than free recall (produce it from nothing).

**How to apply:** retrieval is the main verb of every chunk. Quiz *before* re-explaining. Prefer "tell me
what X is" over "is X true/false?". When the learner is stuck, give a cue, not the answer.

---

## 2. Spacing effect / the forgetting curve
**Finding (Ebbinghaus, 1885; distributed-practice literature):** memory decays exponentially after learning,
but each *spaced* review flattens the curve. Reviews spread over days beat the same number of reviews crammed
into one session ("massed" practice).

**Why:** each retrieval after partial forgetting is more effortful and therefore more strengthening; spacing
also ties the memory to more varied contexts.

**How to apply:** generate retrieval cards and schedule expanding reviews (e.g. day 1 / 3 / 7 / 16). Put at
least one review after sleep. Visualize the decay + review markers in the dashboard.

---

## 3. Desirable difficulties
**Finding (Bjork):** conditions that make learning feel *harder* and slower during practice (spacing,
interleaving, retrieval, varied conditions) produce *better* long-term retention and transfer. Conditions
that make it feel easy (re-reading, massing, blocking) produce worse retention but inflated confidence.

**Why:** ease during study is mistaken for learning; the effort of overcoming difficulty is what encodes.

**How to apply:** deliberately choose the harder path — retrieval over re-reading, interleaving over
blocking, spacing over cramming. Warn the learner it will *feel* less smooth; that's the point. Keep
difficulty achievable, though (see ZPD) — desirable, not crushing.

---

## 4. Generation effect
**Finding (Slamecka & Graf, 1978):** information you *generate* yourself is remembered better than the same
information you read. Filling in `b_tt_r` beats reading "butter".

**How to apply:** make the learner produce — finish the sentence, predict the next step, derive the formula —
before you supply it.

---

## 5. Pretesting / errorful generation
**Finding (Kornell, Richland):** attempting to answer *before* learning the material — even when you guess
wrong — improves retention of the eventual answer, versus studying the same time without the guess.

**Why:** the failed retrieval primes the relevant memory structures and creates a "gap" the answer then
fills (see curiosity, prediction error).

**How to apply:** the Phase-0 pretest and the per-chunk "predict" step. Don't fear wrong guesses — they help.
The per-chunk predict step is core to the loop and shouldn't be skipped. The *upfront* pretest is the
recommended opener but may be skipped on request (some learners just want the artifact) — if skipped, fall
back to a quick self-rating to calibrate rather than guessing blind.

---

## 6. Interleaving
**Finding (Rohrer & Taylor):** mixing different problem types within a practice set ("interleaving") beats
practicing one type to mastery before moving on ("blocking"), especially for learning to *discriminate*
between cases and for transfer.

**How to apply:** once two+ chunks exist, pull questions across them rather than finishing one entirely
first. The final calibration quiz is fully mixed.

---

## 7. Elaborative interrogation & self-explanation
**Finding (Chi et al.; Pressley):** asking "why is this true?" and "how does this connect to what I know?"
(elaborative interrogation), and explaining each step to oneself (self-explanation), markedly improve
comprehension and retention.

**How to apply:** after the reveal, ask "why does this work? where would it break? how does it tie to
[earlier chunk]?" Make the learner articulate the connection, not just confirm it.

---

## 8. The Feynman technique
**Finding (folk method, well-supported by generation + gap-detection research):** explaining a topic in
plain language, as if teaching a beginner, exposes exactly where understanding is thin — the points where
you fall back on jargon or hand-waving.

**How to apply:** the teach-back step. Have the learner explain the chunk simply. Listen for jargon used as a
shield, skipped steps, "it just does X". Those are the gaps — loop back to *only* those.

---

## 9. Dual coding
**Finding (Paivio):** information encoded both verbally and visually is remembered better than either alone —
two retrieval routes, not one.

**How to apply:** pair every verbal model with a visual one. This is why the dashboard exists: the concept
map, Bloom ladder, and forgetting curve give the verbal material a visual partner.

---

## 10. Chunking & schema theory
**Finding (Miller, 1956 — "7±2"; Ausubel; Novak's concept maps):** working memory holds only a handful of
items; experts cope by grouping detail into meaningful "chunks" / schemas. Ausubel: *"the most important
single factor influencing learning is what the learner already knows."*

**How to apply:** compress the subject to 5–9 chunks. Anchor each to the learner's existing schema via
analogy *before* adding detail. The concept map *is* the schema, made visible.

---

## 11. Cognitive Load Theory & the worked-example effect
**Finding (Sweller):** working memory is easily overloaded. Split load into intrinsic (inherent difficulty),
extraneous (poor presentation — minimize this), and germane (effort that builds schemas — maximize this).
For novices, studying *worked examples* beats solving problems unaided; as expertise grows, worked examples
help less and solving helps more (the "expertise reversal").

**How to apply:** for novices, lead with a fully worked example, then a *faded* one (some steps blanked),
then a solo problem — the "completion problem" progression. Strip extraneous clutter. Raise problem-solving
demand as the learner improves.

---

## 12. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) & scaffolding
**Finding (Vygotsky):** the most learning happens just beyond what the learner can currently do alone, with
support ("scaffolding") that is gradually withdrawn.

**How to apply:** calibrate difficulty to *just* past current ability — never far beyond (frustration) or
below (boredom). Scaffold, then fade. The pretest tells you where the edge is.

---

## 13. Prediction error / dopaminergic learning
**Finding (Schultz; Rescorla–Wagner):** the brain learns from the *gap* between what it predicted and what
happened. Dopamine signals prediction error; larger surprise (within reason) drives larger updates.

**How to apply:** the predict-then-reveal step. Force a concrete prediction, then reveal — the surprise is
the teaching moment. A reveal with no prior prediction wastes it.

---

## 14. Curiosity / the information gap
**Finding (Loewenstein, 1994; Gruber et al., 2014):** curiosity arises from a perceived *gap* between what
one knows and wants to know, and states of high curiosity enhance hippocampal learning — even of incidental
material encountered while curious.

**How to apply:** open each chunk with a question or puzzle that creates the gap *before* you fill it. A good
question is a better opener than a good explanation.

---

## 15. Metacognition & the illusion of fluency
**Finding (Dunning–Kruger; judgments-of-learning research):** learners are poorly calibrated — fluent reading
and familiarity feel like knowing, but predict performance badly. The less someone knows, the more they tend
to overestimate.

**How to apply:** make the learner *predict* their score, then show the actual. Treat "I get it" as a
hypothesis to test, never a result. The honest gap is the most useful output of the session.

---

## 16. Bloom's taxonomy
**Finding (Bloom, 1956; revised Anderson & Krathwohl):** learning objectives form a hierarchy of cognitive
demand: **remember → understand → apply → analyze → evaluate → create**.

**How to apply:** set the target rung in Phase 0 (memorizing terms is a different job from being able to
*build* with the idea) and track progress up the ladder. Don't test at "remember" if the goal is "apply".

---

## 17. Memory consolidation in sleep
**Finding:** memories are reactivated and transferred from hippocampus to neocortex during sleep; sleep after
learning measurably improves retention.

**How to apply:** for multi-day goals, schedule at least one spaced review *after* a night's sleep. Tell the
learner that stopping and sleeping is part of the method, not a break from it.

---

## 18. Productive failure
**Finding (Kapur):** having learners attempt to solve a problem *before* being taught the method — and
initially failing — leads to better conceptual understanding and transfer than being taught the method first,
*provided* instruction follows.

**How to apply:** for more capable learners, lead with the struggle (a problem just past their reach), let
them flail productively, *then* instruct. Always follow the failure with the consolidating explanation.

---

## 19. Transfer & analogical reasoning
**Finding (Gick & Holyoak):** knowledge often fails to transfer to new contexts unless the deep structure is
made explicit; giving two analogous cases and prompting comparison promotes far transfer.

**How to apply:** end on transfer — apply the idea to a novel problem. Use analogy to anchor (near) and to
generalize (far). Name the deep structure shared across cases, not just the surface story.

## Putting it together: the per-chunk loop maps to the science

| Curiosity gap | curiosity / information gap (14) |
